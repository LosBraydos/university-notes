Protein structure:
- Peptide bond is planar (amide bond has double bond characteristics and resonance)
- Gives rigidity to the peptide bond.
- Bonds around the peptide bond can rotate, affecting protein structure
	- C(a)-C bond is called psi
	- N-C(a) bond is called phi
- Rotation is restricted by steric hindrance of the R-groups
- The possible combinations of psi and phi can be plotted on a Ramachandran plot:![[Pasted image 20260327100905.png]]

Alpha-helix:
- Characteristics:
	- Hydrogen bond between CO (electronegative) of aa1 and NH (electropositive) of aa5
	- 3.6-amino acids per turn
	- Side chains pointing out
	- Usually 10-20 amino acids (up to 40)
	- Right-handed helices are more common, but left is also possible
	![[Pasted image 20260327101432.png]]
- Formation depends on amino acid content:
	- Process is not fully understood yet.
	- Alanine is a good helix former
	- Positive-negative side chains 3-4 amino acids apart are good helix formers
	- Proline is a helix breaker (rigid structure)
	- Usually negatively charged amino acids towards the N-terminus and opposite for negative
		- Due to dipole moment of the helix

Beta-sheet:
- Characteristics
	- Hydrogen bond between CO and NH that are far apart
	- Up to 15 amino acids
	- Parallell or anti-parallell (refers to direction of peptide chain, i.e. N-to-C or C-to-N)
		- Parallell - Same direction of two beta sheets
		- Anti-parallell - Opposite direction of two beta sheets
	- Beta-sheets do not exist on their own. Exists at least in pairs
	![[Pasted image 20260327102455.png]]
- Formation depends on amino acid content
	- No good rules to predict beta-sheet formation
	- Proline is a sheet breaker
	- At least 4 amino acids between sheets (to allow for turn)
		- Beta-turn: 4 amino acids, often glycine and proline (small and easy to pack)

Tertiary structure (fibrous proteins)
- Insoluble proteins
- Mostly simple secondary structures for long segments
- Example:
	- Keratin (har, horns, nails, skin)
		- Right handed alfa-helix 
		- Results in left-handed superhelix (two helices forming a double helix)
		- Two polypeptides coil around each other, held together by vdW and disulfide bonds
	- Collagen (connective tissue in bones and tendons)
		- Mostly glycine-proline-proline(4-OH) repeats
		- Forms left handed helical structure (rigidity of proline and repulsion by proline side chain)
		- Triple helix from three polypeptide chains
	- Silk
		- Beta-sheets with high glycine and alanine content (small size of side chains allows lots of packing)
		- Very strong within beta-sheets, but between layers weak

Tertiary structure (globular proteins)
- Soluble proteins
- Spherical structure that is a mixture of secondary structures
	- Hydrophobic residues towards inside of structure
	- Hydrophilic residues towards outside
	- Stabilized by vdW and disulfide bonds
- Motifs or folds: segment that has a certain structure and is present in multiple proteins
- Domains: part of the sequence that is independently stable![[Pasted image 20260327103829.png]]

Intrinsically disordered proteins
- Lack a defined structure
- High density of charged amino acids (K, R, D)
- Can be important regions to interact with other proteins, where they then take an ordered structure

Quaternary structure
- Association of two or more polypeptides (each folded into its tertiary structure)
- Complexes of multiple proteins

Protein folding
- Usually one amino acid sequence leads to one tertiary structure
- Normally polypeptides begin folding as they exit the ribosome, while they are still being assembled.
- Spontaneous folding
	- Generally only small proteins does this
- Folding pathway and molecular chaperones
	- Possible conformations for a polypeptide with >100 aa is astronomically large!
	- Folding pathway: first a molten globule then slowly nudged to the right conformation
	- Aided by molecular chaperones
		- Hsp70: binds/unbinds hydrophobic regions of unfolded proteins
		- Chaperonins (GroEL): hollowed out tube that threads the protein through to facilitate folding (hydrophobic to hydrophilic transition)

Isoelectric point
- A function of the ionization characterisitcs of the side chain and the amine group at the N-terminus and the carboxylic group at the C-terminus

Molecular weight
- Sum of the molecular weight of all the amino acids
- Can be estimated as around the number of residues * 110 Da

Prosthetic groups: some proteins (conjugated proteins) have other chemical components
- Lipoproteins: lipids
- Glycoproteins: carbohydrates
- Metalloproteins: metals

Working with proteins
- To study a protein you have to purify it
	- Genetic engineering: adding a few amino acids or specific modifications that make it easier to isolate the protein
	- Extraction from cells/tissue
		- Crude extract (certain organelles can be purified), old way
		- Fractionation by a physicochemical characteristic of the protein (e.g. charge, size, solubility)
			- Salting out
			- Dialysis
			- Chromatography 
- Electrophoresis
	- Measure pI (isoelectric focusing)
	- Estimate molecular weight or purity of sample (SDS-PAGE)
		- Proteins are denatured with SDS
		- SDS gives negative charge to proteins (proteins migrate to positive pole)
		- Polyacrylamide creates a mesh through which proteins need to migrate
- Structure determination
	- Determining protein structure
		- X-ray crystallography
		- NMR
		- Cryo-electron microscopy
	- Predicting protein structure
		- AlphaFold
		- RoseTTAFold
		- Methods based on deep learning
