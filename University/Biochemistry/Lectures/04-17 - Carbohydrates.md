- E.q. crown structure on coronavirus is made of carbohydrates
	- Soft, makes it harder for the immune system to bind to and destroy.

Carbs have diverse roles in biology:
- Biochemical fuel (glucose)
- Form, structure, and stability (cellulose, chitin)
- Cell-cell communication
- Pathogen attachment to host cells, immune evasion

What are carbohydrates?
- Polyhydroxylated aldehydes or ketones
- Monosaccharides: monomeric building blocks

Stereoisomery
- Almost all monosaccharides have hydroxyls attached to asymmetric centers.
- Configuration of asymmetric carbons determine the type of monosaccharide - Stereoisomery
- Important for reactions with biological molecules (enzyme specificity)

Fischer projections:
- Convention for drawing monosaccharides
- Recipe:
	- Draw aldehyde or ketone at the top
	- Draw carbon chain straight down until you have desired number of carbons
	- OH-groups go left or right on asymmetric carbons, depending on monosaccharide

 Nomenclature:
 - With aldehydes: aldoses
 - With ketones: ketoses
 - With 3 carbons: aldotriose or ketotriose
 - With 4 carbons: aldotetrose or ketotetrose

Monosaccharides involved in RNA and DNA:
- Ribose and deoxyribose

Derivatives:
- Many monosaccharide derivatives have critical functions in biology:
	- beta-D-Glucose
		- N-Acetyl-beta-D-glucosamine
		- beta-D-Glucose 6-phosphate

Haworth projection is a common approximation. Remember that sugars are not actually planar, they assume boat and chair conformations like cyclohexane. Chair is the lowest energy conformation.

Oligosaccharides:
- Monosaccharides can undergo condensation reactions to form oligosaccharides.
- O-glycosidic bond
- Example:
	- alpha-Glc reacts with another Glc: forms an alpha-1,4-glycosidic bond.
- Some common disaccharides:
	- Glucose + Fructose: Sucrose (alpha-1,2-glycosidic bond)
	- Galactose + Glucose: Lactose (beta-1,4-glycosidic bond)
	- Glucose + Glucose: Maltose (alpha-1,4-glycosidic bond)

Polysaccharides:
- Branched and unbranched
- Homopolysaccharides:
	- Made of one type of monomer
- Heteropolysaccharides:
	- Made of two or more different types of monomers
- Vital roles in energy storage and structural integrity

Why are nutritional reserves stored as polysaccharides?
- Due to osmosis, it is better to store saccharides as large polymers, giving fewer individual particles in solution than as individual monomers.
- If glucose were monomeric, osmotic pressure would be so high cells would explode (lysis)

Most common polysaccharides:
- Glycogen
	- Animal storage of glucose
	- Glycogen is branched with alpha-1,4 and alpha-1,6 linkages
	- Twice as many branching points as amylopectin
- Starch
	- Plant storage of glucose
	- Amylose - unbranched
	- Amylopectin - branched at alpha-1,6 linkages
- Cellulose
	- Unbranched glucose polymer with beta-1,4 bonds
	- Dense fibers with high tensile strength
	- Network of hydrogen bonding between neighbouring cellulose chains
	- High rigidity and stability!
- Chitin
	- Second most abundant polysaccharide in nature
	- Very similar to cellulose, with modified monomers
		- Unbranched N-acetylglucosamine polymer with beta-1,4 linkages
		- Glucose with substituted -OH

Glycoproteins:
- Carbohydrates can covalently bond to proteins
- Often components of cell membranes
	- The carbohydrate usually faces outside
- Sugar chains attached to glycoproteins are called **glycans**
	- Can be attached via:
		- Nitrogen of asparagine (N-linked glycans)
		- Oxygen of serine or threonine (O-linked glycans)
- Glycobiology uses sugar code for notation.
- Huge diversity, important roles in:
	- Cell-cell recognition and adhesion
		- Protein stability, folding, and solubility
		- Self/non-self discrimination
		- Pathogen binding

Example:
- Lectins:
	- Lectins are proteins that bind and recognize specific glycans, "reads" the sugar code
	- Targets immune cells to where they are needed
- Blood groups
	- ABO systems: glycans on blood cell surface
	- We make antibodies targeting glycans we do not possess, e.g. anti-A targets A

Summary:
- Carbohydrates are polyhydroxylated aldehydes and ketones
- Stereoisomery can determine sugar properties and reactivity (glucose, mannose, galactose...)
- Monosaccharides form oligomers via alpha and beta glycosidic bonds
- Polysaccharides include important nutrient storage and structural molecules
- The linkages between subunits determine the structural properties (e.g. glycogen vs. cellulose)
- Proteins can be covalently modified with glycans, glycoproteins
- Carbohydrates on proteins have remarkably diversity
- Glycans have important functions including cell-cell communication
- Lectins are proteins that recognize specific glycans and "read" the sugar code
