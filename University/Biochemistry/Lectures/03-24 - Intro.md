There are three domains of life:
- Bacteria
- Archaea
- Eukarya
	- Animals, fungi, plants, and more...
Different cellular structures.

Eukarya store the genome in the nucleus.
Bacteria and Archaea (prokaryotes) have no nucleus.

Archaea inhabit extreme environments (high pressure, high temperature, high acidity).

Cells:
- Bacterial cells are much smaller (order of 1 µm) than animal cells (20-50 µm)
- Eukaryotes can be multicellular, but also single celled. 
- Most important distinction: Eukaryotes have organelles, separating metabolic processes.

The plasma membrane defines the periphery of the cell:
- Composed of lipids and protein molecules.
- Thin and flexible. Hydrophobic barrier around the cell.

The cytoplasm is the internal volume enclosed in plasma membrane

Gram positive/negative bacteria:
- Positive:
	- Colored by Gram's stain
	- Thick peptidoglycan layer outside plasma membrane, what gets stained.
- Negative:
	- Outer membrane composed of a lipid bilayer

The animal cell:
![[Pasted image 20260324133408.png]]


- Nucleus:
	- Contains the DNA.
	- Transcription (DNA -> RNA)
	- Nuclear pores - facilitates transport in and out
	- Has two membranes: an inner and an outer
	
- Mitochondria:
	- ATP-production
	- Inner and outer membranes, with and intermembrane space. Within the inner membrane is the matrix.
	- Has its own DNA
	- Very similar to bacteria --> endosymbiont hypothesis
- Endosymbiosis:
	- Evidence:
		- Mitochondria have their own ribosomes that resemble bacterial ribosomes.
		- Inne membrane resembles bacterial membrane
		- Antibiotics affect mitochondria
		- Mitochondria have their own genetic material that is similar to proteobacteria.

Metabolites and macromolecules:
- Biochemistry:
	- Proteins
	- Nucleic acids
	- Polysaccharides
	- Building blocks and metabolites (lipids, amino acids, carbohydrates, etc.)
	- Reoccurring theme: long chains of chemical building blocks are assembled into large macromolecules.

- Proteins - Long polymers of amino acids:
	- Can function as enzymes, structural elements, signal receptors, transporters.
	- Complex 3D structure, important for their function.
	
- Nucleic acids - DNA/RNA, polymers of nucleotides.
	- Store and transmit genetic information
	- Some RNA have structural and catalytic roles in supramolecular complexes. 
	
- Polysaccharides - Polymers of simple sugars
	- Energy-rich fuel
	- Rigid structural components (e.g. cellulose) of cell walls.
	- Binds to proteins on other cells
	
- Lipids - water-insoluble hydrocarbon derivates
	- Energy-rich
	- Structural components of membranes

Bioenergetics:
- Thermodynamics - Entropy of the universe will always increase.
	- Heat flows from warm to cold
	- "Disorder" - Energy dispersal.
	- Probability of states. There are many more "disordered" states than "ordered" states. 
- Living organisms operate within the laws of thermodynamics!
- Living systems make transactions of energy and material with the outside to maintain "order" within the system. 
- Important: gibby

- ATP is used to make thermodynamically unfavourable reactions favourable to make them occur spontaneously.	![[Pasted image 20260324135741.png]]


- Dynamic steady-state:
	- Living organisms exist in a dynamic steady state - homeostasis.
	- Energy is taken from surroundings to maintain homeostasis.
	- Almost all energy obtained by a cell comes from redox reactions (flow of electrons)
	- NOT an equilibrium. A cell at equilibrium is dead.
	- Energy is consumed to fight against equilibrium.
- Energy sources:
	- Phototrophs - trap and use sunlight
	- Chemotrophs - derive energy from oxidation of chemical fuel
	- Autotrophs - can synthesize all necessary biomolecules from CO2
	- Heterotrophs - require some preformed organic nutrients (from external sources)
