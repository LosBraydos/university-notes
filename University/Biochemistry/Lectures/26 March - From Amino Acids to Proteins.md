Proteins have many functions:
- Contraction (muscle)
- Provide structure (e.g. cytoskeleton)
- Catalyze metabolic reactions (enzymes)
- Signaling
- Transport
- Hormones (e.g. insulin)
- Defence (antibodies)
- And many more...

Proteins are biomolecules composed of amino acids (peptides/polypeptides/proteins)
- From less than 50 to over 30 000 (titin)
- Same general structure, amino group + carboxylic acid and a side chain

There are 20 different amino acids
- Full name (e.g. cysteine)
- 3-letter abbreviation (Cys)
- 1-letter abbreviation, for bioinformatics (C)
- Learn the structures and abbreviations!

9 essential amino acids for humans:
- Cannot be synthesized
- K, M, W, V, F, I, L, T, H 
- **K**ind **M**en and **W**omen **V**acuum **FILTH**

Each amino acid is an enantiomer (glycine is the only achiral amino acid)
- Only L-amino acids are present in proteins
	- D-amino acids are present in bacterial cell walls

All amino acids have ionizable groups:
- At physiological pH (7.4), most amino acids are zwitterionic.
- The pKa of the amino acid can tell the charge in physiological pH
	- pKa < 7.4 => +
	- pKa > 7.4 => -
- Side chains can be protonated or deprotonated depending on environment. (look this up)

Polarity
- Serine and threonine, hydroxyl group
- Cysteine, thiol group
- Asparagine and glutamine, amides
- Polar groups (and ionized) are hydrophilic
- Non-polar groups are hydrophobic
	- Affects protein structure
	- Gets buried inside protein structure, away from water

Modifications
- Two extra amino acids are coded for in the genetic code, but are not commonly used
	- Selenocysteine (important in some enzymes, but rare)
	- Pyrrolysine (only in archaea and some bacteria)
- Amino acids can be modified post-translation, e.g.
	- Hydroxyl (-OH)
	- Methyl (-CH3)
	- Phosphate (-PO4^3-)
	- Glycosylation (different sugars)
	- Ubiquitination (ubiquitin, 8kDa polypeptides that signals degradation)
	- Lipidation
	- Many more...

Disulfide bonds:
- Cysteine can form disulfide bonds via oxidation. 
- The -SH group form a S-S bridge between different cysteine.

Protein synthesis:
- Polypeptides are polymers of amino acids
- Peptide bonds are formed via an amide bond, a condensation of amino group and carboxyl group.
- Synthesis occurs in the N to C direction
	- Every protein has an N-terminus and C-terminus![[Pasted image 20260326111426.png]]
- Level of structure
	- Primary: amino acid sequence
	- Secondary: conformation of different parts of polypeptide (e.g. helices, sheets, turns)
	- Tertiary: 3D configuration of the protein
	- Quaternary: association between different polypeptides to form a multi-subunit protein

Sequence
- Amino acid sequence contains the information for its structure and function
	- Comparing sequence of a new protein with sequences of a known can suggest its functions
	- Certain sequences (consensus sequences) are associated with specific functions

Amino acid sequence folds into 3D structure driven by intermolecular interactions
- Weak interactions: electrostatic interactions, hydrogen bonds, van der Waals interactions...
- Folding of a protein decreases enthalpy, the formation of bonds is favourable and releases heat.
	- However electrostatic interactions and hydrogen bonds between amino acids and water generally puts the net stability contribution to zero.
- Protein folding decreases entropy, limiting possible configurations
- But entropy of the water is increased as hydrophobic groups are buried inside the protein allowing freedom of water.
