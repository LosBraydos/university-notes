Enzymes are Powerful Biological Catalysts
- They increase reaction rates, making life possible
- Basically all biochemical reactions are enzyme-mediated
- Almost all are proteins
- A quarter of our genes encode for enzymes
- Enzymes are highly specific
- Many drugs target enzymes

Enzymes are fast! Example:
- Carbonic anhydrase catalyzes hydration of CO2, crucial to maintain blood pH![[Pasted image 20260330100750.png|426]]

Enzymes are highly specific! Example:
- Proteases catalyse the hydrolysis of peptide bonds (degrades proteins)
- Trypsin only hydrolysis peptide bonds after a Lys/Arg amino acid
- Thrombin only does this after Arg

Enzyme names (nomenclature)
- Normally the names reflect the reaction they catalyse, with suffix "-ase" with prefix of what they do.
- Example: aminotransfer**ase**, catalyzes transfer of -NH2.
- However, historical names are often used instead...

Many enzymes require cofactors
- Many enzymes use metal ions or small organic molecules to aid catalysis
- Look out for the nomenclature
	- Cofactors are metals or small molecules
	- Conenzymes are only small molecules![[Pasted image 20260330101511.png]]

Catalysis
- Enzymes cannot alter the laws of thermodynamics
- Enzymes do not affect the equilibrium or the free energy exchange of a reaction
- They do however affect how fast the equilibrium is reached, they work just as well in the reverse direction as the forward direction

Active site:
- Enzymes binds their substrates in a way favourable to the formation of the transition state.
- The area this occurs is the *active site* of the enzyme.
![[Pasted image 20260330102252.png|402]]
- The active site is a three-dimensional pocket formed by the protein
- Within the active site, amino acids form a microenvironment that facilitates the catalyzed reaction
- Specificity comes from the shape and intermolecular interactions in the active site that together only fit certain substrates

Induced fit
- Enzymes are often flexible and not rigid structures.
- The active site can therefore reshape to fit substrates
- They are complementary only after binding

Enzyme kinetics
- The study of reaction rates of enzyme-catalyzed reactions

Michaelis-Menten kinetics
- Simplified model
- Applicable for many enzymes
- A general enzyme-catalyzed reaction:![[Pasted image 20260330102951.png|455]]
- kcat/KM is a measure for catalytic efficiency
	- kcat is a measure for rate of catalysis
	- KM can be a measure for affinity of E to S
	- Both are enzyme-specific constants
	- kcat/KM takes into account rate of catalysis and strength of ES interaction

Enzymes can be inhibited with certain compounds
- Inhibitors are crucial in medicine as therapeutics
- Examples:
	- Penicillin: inhibits enzyme involved in bacterial cell-wall synthesis
	- Omeprazole: inhibits gastric H+/K+-ATPase
	- Aspirin: inhibition of prostaglandin synthesis
	- Saquinavir: inhibition of protease required for HIV maturation
	- ....
1. Reversible inhibitors
	- Competitive inhibitors
		- Binds at the exact same active site as a substrate and blocks it. Both cannot be bound at the same time
		- Competitive inhibition can be overcome by adding more substrate
		- Same vmax can be reached, but KM gets higher (more S required to reach vmax/2)
	- Uncompetitive inhibitors
		- Inhibitor only binds to ES complex.
		- Some amount of ES is bound to inhibitor, gets taken out of equation.
		- Same vmax is never reached.
		- KM is lowered (higher affinity) as inhibitor traps ES, making enzyme appear to hold on to substrate more tightly
	- Non-competitive inhibitors
		- Binds at a completely different site than the active site. Can bind to both free enzyme and ES complex
		- Enzymes are disabled by inhibitor
		- Decreased vmax. However, KM stays the same as proportion of ES and E don't change
		- No shift to apparent higher affinity
2. Irreversible inhibitors
	- Bind covalently with or destroy a functional group on an enzyme. Makes the enzyme useless
	- Example: Penicillin
		- Covalently modifies a serine sidechain in the active site of bacterial transpeptidase.

Allostery
- MM kinetics is a powerful tool to quantify enzyme catalysis
- But it breaks down for "allosteric" enzymes
- An allosteric enzyme has multiple active sites and binding of one substrate molecule changes the properties of the other active sites
- The substrate acts as activity regulator
- Additionally, further regulatory molecules can bind to allosteric enzymes, changing the activity
- Important in metabolism, where metabolic circumstances change (e.g. nutrient availability). More on that in metabolism
