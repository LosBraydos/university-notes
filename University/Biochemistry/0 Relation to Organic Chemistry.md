#### Resonance
Adjacent sp2-hybridized atoms (usually carbon, oxygen, and nitrogen) will have overlapping p-orbitals. This leads to a delocalized π-cloud that encompasses all the involved atoms. The charge is spread out over the molecule, making it more stable. Explains important reactions such as the acidity of carboxylic acids.
In biochemical reactions, intermediates are often stabilized by resonance and resonance can be used to explain why certain parts of a molecule may preferentially react.

#### Nucleophilicity and Electrophilicity
- Nucleophiles are electron-rich, they tend to donate electrons to a bond
- Electrophiles are electron-poor, they tend to accept electrons to form a bond

A higher charge density makes a better nucleophile, while resonance spreads the charge making poorer nucleophiles. Bulkiness give rise to steric hindrance, physically blocking nucleophiles from reaction and therefore makes them weaker. Often oxygens and nitrogens act as nucleophiles in biochemical reactions.

An electrophile is often positively charged or surrounded (bound) by electron-withdrawing groups. A classic example is the carbon in carbonyls.
