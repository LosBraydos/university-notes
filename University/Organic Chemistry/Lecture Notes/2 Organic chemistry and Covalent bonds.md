- Bond angles depend on whether it's a single, double, or triple bond
- Carbons and hydrogens are normally implicitly shown by lines - a skeletal formula
- One bond represent two shared electrons

Orbitals (s and p):
- Orbitals of same phase can combine to create a lower energy covalent bond
- Orbitals of opposite phase destructively interfere to create a nodal plane, an area where no electrons will be found - a higher energy anti-bond
- This is summarised in a MO diagram
- In carbon, 2s; 2px; 2py; and 2pz participate in bonding. The individual atomic orbitals can combine into hybrid orbitals - hybridisation. 
	- C-H is formed by interaction between H 1s and C sp3. Four of these bonds are formed in CH4
		- Bond angles in CH4 are 109.5 degrees, forming a tetrahedral structure
	- If only two p-orbitals hybridise with s, sp2 is formed and one p-orbital is left over which can interact with another p-orbital to form a pi-bond, a double bond. The bond angle will be 120 degrees
	- Similarly, sp (one s and one p) can be formed leaving two p-orbitals which can combine with another atom's to form a triple bond, or two separate double bonds. The bond will be 180 degrees
	- Bond lengths and energies depend on hybridisation
		- Single: 1.54 Å, 347 kJ/mol
		- Double: 1.33 Å, 598 kJ/mol
		- Triple: 1.20 Å, 812 kJ/mol
	- Conjugation influence stability and reactivity
	- Acidity of hydrocarbons
		- Ethane (an alkane) has a pKa of 45
		- Ethene has pKa 36
		- Ethyne has pKa 25



When an atom bonds to four different groups stereo-isomerism can occur. 

The rigidity of pi-bonds prevents free rotation which leads to cis-trans isomerism.
```smiles
Cl\C=C/Cl
Cl/C=C/Cl
```
Formal charge:
- The formal charge is calculated by the number of electrons around the atoms and the amount of valence electrons it normally has. 
- Knowing the formal charge of an atom is important for what reactions are possible.
```smiles  
  [O+]([H])([H])[H]
  ```
  Electronegativity:
  - A more electronegative atom will draw more electrons towards itself in a bond creating a dipole moment.
  - An important example is an electronegative element like oxygen bonded to carbon. The carbon would become more electron-poor.
