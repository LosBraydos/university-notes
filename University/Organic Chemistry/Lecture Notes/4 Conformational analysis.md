# Stereochemistry

Mirrored isomers of a compound, arises when a carbon has four *different* substituents. Chiral molecules. The two isomers of a chiral molecule are called enantiomers to each other. ![[Pasted image 20260122104025.png]]
A characteristic for stereoisomers is that they can rotate plane polarised light in different orientation. For the same reason they are sometimes called optical isomers and optically active. This can also be used to determine the stereochemistry of a sample as light is rotated in opposite directions by the enantiomers. 

In order to determine the absolute configuration of an enantiomer, the different substituents are prioritized according to atomic number, followed by number of bonds to other substituents accounting for atomic number.

dia-stereoisomers: 

In general there are 2^n stereoisomers where n is the number of stereogenic centra. 
Inner symmetry plane will give a meso-compound that means certain compounds will have fewer stereoisomers than expected, such as tartaric acid.

When molecules are synthesized you might get an even mixture of enantiomers, a racemic mixture. There are different ways to separate a racemic mixture.
