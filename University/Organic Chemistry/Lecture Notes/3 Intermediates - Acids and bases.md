Three basic mechanisms for bonds breaking:
1. Heterolytically: both electrons in the covalent bond stay with one fragment.
2. Homolytically: each fragment retains one electron, typical for radicals
3. Reactions where al bonds are broken and formed in one step, such as pericyclic reactions:

Ions and radicals formed as intermediates in multi step reactions are often short lived.
Understanding these intermediates are important to understand reaction mechanisms

The most common intermediates are:
- Carbocations
- Radicals
- Carbanions
"Rich give to poor" - electron-rich atoms will give to electron-poor atoms.

Carbocations are sp2-hybridised carrying an empty p-orbital, a planar configuration.
The stability of carbocations increase with increasing alkyl substitutions. Alkyl have an electron-donating effect which stabilises the cation. Carbocations can be more stabilised through resonance as well. This is due to charge density decreasing as charge is distributed over several atoms.

Carbanions are normally sp3-hybridised, except when in resonance structures in which case they are sp2-hybridised. (in order to overlap with pi-system)
Stability order is reversed compared with cations and radicals for the same reason; electron donating groups further *increase* electron density which destabilises the anion.
Resonance stabilises anions as well by reducing overall charge density.

Basic reactions types:
1. Substitution reactions
	- Bonds can be formed and broken at the same time or happen in multiple steps with intermediates.
2. Addition reactions
3. Elimination reactions
4. Rearrangement reactions
5. Redox reactions (heterogenic, can sometimes be categorised as above instead)
6. Acid-base reactions

### Acid-base reactions

Brönsted-Lowry: Acids are proton donators and bases are proton acceptors
Lewis: Acids are electron-pair acceptors and bases are electron-pair donors.
According to the Lewis definition a proton is an electron-pair acceptor, making it an acid which further means that the Brönsted-Lowry definition is a special case of the Lewis definition.
Acid-base reactions reach equilibrium which is summarised by the equilibrium constant (Ka or Kb). A high Ka means a lot of products with respect to reactants and a low Ka means few products at equilibrium. 
A stable anion after deprotonation means a stronger acid with a lower pKa value. 
General trends show that weaker XH bonds and lower charge density of the anion leads to a more stable anion.
