MS, IR, and NMR

Mass Spectrometry:
Sample is ionised:
- From gas:
	- Electron impact ionisations (EI)
	- Chemical ionisation (CI) - plasma
- From solid:
	- Laser desorption (LD)
	- Matrix assisted laser desorption ionisation (MALDI)
- Liquid phase:
	- Electrospray ionisation (ESI)
	- Inductively coupled plasma (ICP)

If needed the sample is separated into different parts using chromatography.
There are many more techniques besides the ones mentioned.

The charged ions are passed through a mass analyser, which measures how fast the ions move. Lighter ions will move faster than heavier ions and is dependent on mass-to-charge ratio. Another way is detecting deflection against a magnetic field. 

As the molecule is being ionised it can fragment which will display as multiple peaks on a mass spectra. In some MS it is possible to choose whether the ion is to be fragmented or not.

Because MS measures the mass of the ion, elements with several stable isotopes have to be taken into account. 
- X+1 elements ($^{12}$C, $^{13}$C, $^{14}$N, N-15, etc.) 
	- Two stable isotopes exist, second is X+1
	- Signal intensity depends on relative abundance of isotope (C-12 = 98.89 %, C-13, 1.11 %)
- X+2 elements (Cl-35/37, Br-79-81)
	- Abundance for Cl is roughly 75:25
	- Br is roughly 50:50

Fragmentation is important for structure determination. Different fragmentation methods yield different fragments that can be used to infer the structure of a molecule. 

Infrared Spectroscopy:
Observe the vibrations of molecular bonds using infrared light. Different frequencies of IR-light are absorbed by different types of bonds, increasing their energy and amplitude of their vibrations. The measure of absorption at a given frequency can be used to determine molecular structure. The structure is not revealed directly by the spectra, as molecules with the same type of bonds but different amounts (e.g. heptane vs. octane) give nearly identical spectra.

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance:
Atomic nuclei interact with strong magnetic fields. This can be used to get information on the chemical environment surrounding the atom. Most common types of NMR is $^{1}$H and $^{13}$C NMR.

$^{1}$H NMR:
What information?
1. Number of peaks
	- The amount of protons with *unique* chemical environments.
2. Size of peaks
	- Proportional to number of protons in each peak
3. Splitting pattern
	- How many neighbouring protons there are.
4. Peak position 
	- "Chemical shift", the chemical environment, determined by how "electron-rich" the protons are. Protons bonded to highly electronegative atoms/groups are "shielded" and experience higher chemical shift. 
For example, the protons in a methyl group, -CH3, will all be in the same peak as the free rotation of the group means they will experience the same chemical environment. 

$^{13}$C NMR:
Chemical shift corresponds to what the carbon is bonded to, e.g. C=O (-one), C=O (-al), C=O (acid), C=C (aromatic).
